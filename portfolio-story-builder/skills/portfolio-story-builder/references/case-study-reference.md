# Case Study Reference

This file holds the narrative skeleton, the ranking rubric, and the gap-interview
questions. Read it during Phases 3–5. The SKILL.md body explains *when*; this
explains *what good looks like*.

---

## The story arc

A portfolio piece is not a gallery of images — it's an argument that the
designer can think. Every strong design case study answers, in order:

1. **Hook** — one hero image + one line that positions the project. ("A trail
   runner built around a single-piece knit upper and a 4mm-drop carbon plate.")
2. **Context** — what was being solved, for whom, under what constraints. Brand,
   season, price target, performance spec, manufacturing reality. Constraints
   are not boring; they're what makes the resolution impressive.
3. **Role** — what *this designer* did versus the team. Recruiters and clients
   read this first. "Led upper design and CMF; collaborated with the tooling
   engineer on the outsole" beats "designed a shoe."
4. **Direction** — the conceptual seed. Trend read, materials exploration,
   references, competitive gap. Where the idea came from.
5. **Process** — sketch → digital → 3D → sample → revision. This is the
   workflow proof, and it's exactly the part most designers under-document.
   Treat surviving WIP assets as evidence and reconstruct the sequence. If the
   connective tissue is missing, that's a gap-interview question, not a guess.
6. **Craft & decisions** — the *why* behind the choices, in whatever the
   project's core discipline is. For a physical product that's CMF (color /
   material / finish), construction, and tooling. For a tool, app, or software
   project it's the engineering, UX, and architecture decisions — the node graph,
   the data flow, the interaction model. Match the vocabulary to the work.
   "Switched the collar foam from EVA to a dual-density PU to hold the ankle
   without bulk" and "moved the panel conversion into Geometry Nodes so it stays
   procedural and non-destructive" are the same kind of trust-earning sentence in
   different domains.
7. **Resolution** — final hero shots, in-context / on-body / on-foot, detail
   callouts.
8. **Impact** — did it ship, sell, win, get worn, change a line plan? If the
   outcome is unknown or it never shipped, frame the *intended* outcome honestly
   rather than inventing results.

Not every project has all eight in equal measure. A concept project may be
heavy on 4–6 and light on 8; a shipped product may be light on 5 because the
sketches are long gone. Build the arc the evidence supports, and name what's
missing rather than papering over it.

## Ranking rubric (Phase 3)

Ranking is a **proposal**, not a verdict. Score each candidate project on these
five dimensions, show the scores and the one-line reasoning, and let the user
arbitrate — they know their own work and goals better than any rubric. Use a
simple 1–5 per dimension and a short rationale; do not over-engineer this into
false precision.

- **Visual strength** — are the *final* assets presentation-grade, or would they
  need reshooting/re-rendering before they could front a deck?
- **Story completeness** — is there enough material to build a real arc, or is
  it orphan finals with no process and no context?
- **Distinctiveness** — does it show range or a signature? Does it stand apart
  from the other projects, or is it the third near-identical trail shoe?
- **Skill demonstration** — does it prove a capability the user wants to be
  hired/known for? (For a designer moving toward service-based creative/technical
  work, projects showing 3D, technical development, or end-to-end ownership may
  carry extra weight — but confirm, don't assume.)
- **Relevance** — does it fit where the user is headed, not just where they've
  been? Stale work can still earn a place if it's the best proof of a skill.

Present the ranking as a table the user can react to. Expect them to override —
that's the point. A piece they're emotionally attached to stays in even if it
scores a 3; a flawless render of a forgettable project might get cut.

## The gap interview (Phase 4–5)

The user has said documentation is their weak spot, so the assets will almost
always be missing the *narrative* connective tissue. The skill's most valuable
move is to **excavate it** — not invent it. After auditing a project's assets,
ask the smallest set of questions needed to complete the arc. Typically 3–6 per
project, concrete and answerable, e.g.:

- "What was the actual brief here — who was it for and what was the hard
  constraint?"
- "Which parts of this were yours versus the team's?"
- "Walk me through one decision you're proud of and what you rejected to get
  there."
- "Did this ship? Any numbers, reception, or where it ended up?"
- "These three sketches — are they early exploration or post-feedback revisions?
  I want to sequence them right."

Ask in plain language, batch them, and let the answers — not assumptions — fill
the gaps. If the user genuinely doesn't remember, frame honestly ("Concept
project, never produced") rather than fabricating outcomes.

Also surface **asset gaps** as a concrete shot/render list: "This story is strong
but has no in-context shot — a single on-foot 3/4 would close it," or "No process
survives for the outsole; if you can find the original concept board it makes the
craft section land." Actionable, specific, prioritized.

## Tone

Write in the user's register — a working designer's voice, not marketing copy.
Specific and confident, never inflated. "Solved a packing problem" not
"revolutionized the outdoor space." The work should sound like it was made by
someone who knows what they're doing, because it was.
