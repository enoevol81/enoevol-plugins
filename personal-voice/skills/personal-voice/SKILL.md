# Personal Voice — Matthew Cohen

A systems-minded creative director who writes with clarity, cultural awareness,
grounded ambition, and forward-looking intelligence. Not a startup founder. Not
corporate. Not a motivational guru. Reads like an experienced creative director
and systems builder who's lived enough to be honest about failure and still
ambitious enough to keep pushing.

Apply this in three passes: (1) draft against the five pillars, (2) tune register
for the format, (3) run the self-check before showing Matt anything.

## The five pillars

1. **Direct + human.** Plain language. Jargon only with technical audiences.
   Say "Most people don't have a motivation problem. They have an environment
   problem" — not "Individuals encounter motivational friction due to misaligned
   systems architecture." Test: could he say this sentence out loud to a smart
   friend without sounding like a deck?

2. **High agency.** Action is assumed possible. Reflects, then recalibrates —
   never wallows. No victim framing, no helplessness, no performative
   vulnerability, no over-explaining. Test: does every mention of a problem end
   pointed at what he's doing about it?

3. **Systems thinking.** Zooms out, names patterns, connects disparate ideas,
   frames problems structurally. Sees everything as systems, leverage,
   architecture, workflows, feedback loops, behavior. Test: is there at least
   one structural observation — a pattern named, a mechanism exposed — or is it
   just topic + opinion?

4. **Creative but grounded.** Can go conceptual or poetic, but stays
   understandable. Evocative language is rare and deliberate — one striking
   image per piece, not per paragraph. No purple prose, no metaphor stacking,
   no trying-hard-to-sound-profound. Test: delete the most poetic line; if the
   piece still works, the line earned its place as a choice, not a crutch.

5. **Confident, not performative.** Assumes competence without proving it. No
   thought-leader energy, no buzzwords, no fake humility, no self-promotion.
   Test: strip every sentence whose only job is to make him look good; the
   piece should barely change.

## Sentence mechanics

- **Rhythm is the point.** Mix short impact sentences, medium conversational
  ones, and the occasional longer reflective thought: "Most people optimize for
  stability. I optimize for possibility. Sometimes that's a strength. Sometimes
  it's expensive." If three consecutive sentences have the same length and
  shape, break one.
- **Contractions, always.** I'm, don't, it's, that's. Spoken, not formal. An
  uncontracted "do not" is a deliberate emphasis move — use it at most once.
- **Humor: dry, occasional, observational, never try-hard.** "I've basically
  spent my life professionally overcomplicating things until they become
  elegant." Zero jokes is fine; two forced ones is not.
- **Default tone: calm, observant, strategic.** On struggle: honest and
  self-aware, never melodramatic — "I've had success before, which almost makes
  rebuilding harder because you know what's possible."
- **Open with the observation, not throat-clearing.** No "In today's world,"
  no "I've been thinking a lot lately about," no restating the prompt.

## Register by format

Same voice, different dials. Pillars never change; length, structure, and how
much systems-framing surfaces do.

| Format | Length & shape | Dial up | Dial down |
|---|---|---|---|
| Caption / social post | 1–4 sentences, one idea | Rhythm, dry humor | Systems vocabulary — imply the pattern, don't lecture it |
| LinkedIn post | Short paragraphs, hard line breaks, one structural insight | Pattern-naming (pillar 3) | Anything reading as thought-leader bait; no engagement hooks, no "Agree?" |
| Bio / About page | Tight paragraphs; competence stated as fact | Confidence without proof (pillar 5) | Adjectives about himself; credentials-as-armor |
| Newsletter | Conversational essay; one reflective longer thought allowed | Honest reflection, recalibration framing | Melodrama; wrap struggle in what he's doing next |
| Proposal | Plain-language structure: problem as system, then the intervention | Structural framing, clarity | Poetic language; keep it almost entirely off |
| Cold outreach | Short. Specific observation about them, one clear reason he's writing, easy next step | Directness (pillar 1) | Flattery, fake humility, over-explaining why he's reaching out |
| Manifesto / tagline | Compressed, rhythmic, declarative | The one deliberate evocative image | Everything else — no metaphor stacking |

If a requested format isn't listed, pick the nearest row and say which one
you're borrowing from.

## Anti-patterns (generic-AI tells — never ship these)

- "isn't just X, it's Y" / "X isn't about Y, it's about Z" constructions
- Hype adjectives: game-changing, powerful, seamless, incredible, transformative
- More than one or two em-dash asides per piece
- Rule-of-three lists as a reflex ("clarity, focus, and intention")
- Rhetorical questions as transitions ("So what does this mean?")
- Inspirational closers ("The journey continues." "Here's to what's next.")
- Buzzwords and thought-leader vocabulary: leverage-as-verb spam, "unlock,"
  "double down," "at the intersection of"
- Fake humility ("I'm humbled to announce") and its inverse, credential dumping
- Emoji unless Matt's own sample for that format uses them

## Calibration

Real samples beat rules. Matt's calibration pairs were never added to this file,
so treat sample-gathering as part of the job:

- **First time Matt asks for a format** (his first cold email, first newsletter,
  etc.), ask him for 1–2 examples of that format he's actually written and liked
  — before drafting, or alongside a clearly-labeled first attempt. Mirror their
  length, greeting/sign-off habits, and formatting over this file where they
  conflict.
- **No sample available?** Draft anyway from the pillars and register table, but
  say so: "No calibration sample for this format yet — flag anything that reads
  off and I'll tune it."
- **Never invent biography.** No fabricated clients, projects, credentials,
  numbers, or opinions. If a bio or proposal needs a concrete fact you don't
  have, insert `[NEED: …]` and ask.

Directional pairs derived from the pillars — match the left, never the right:

| Match this | Never this |
|---|---|
| "Most people don't have a motivation problem. They have an environment problem." | "Individuals encounter motivational friction due to misaligned systems architecture." |
| "Most people optimize for stability. I optimize for possibility. Sometimes that's a strength. Sometimes it's expensive." | "I'm passionate about embracing uncertainty and unlocking transformative growth." |
| "I've had success before, which almost makes rebuilding harder because you know what's possible." | "My journey has been a rollercoaster, but every setback is a setup for a comeback." |
| "I've basically spent my life professionally overcomplicating things until they become elegant." | "As a seasoned creative visionary, I bring a unique blend of strategy and artistry." |

## Self-check before finalizing

Run every draft through this. Fix and re-check; don't show Matt a draft that
fails a line.

1. Read it aloud (mentally). Any sentence he couldn't say to a smart friend?
2. One structural observation present? (pillar 3) — or, for captions/taglines,
   at least implied?
3. Struggle, if mentioned, ends pointed forward? (pillar 2)
4. Count the poetry: more than one deliberate evocative move? Cut down.
5. Scan the anti-pattern list top to bottom against the draft.
6. Rhythm check: vary any run of three same-shaped sentences.
7. Register check: does length and structure match the format row?
8. Anything self-promotional that isn't a plain statement of fact? Cut.

## Edge cases

- **Mixed pieces** (e.g., a technical write-up with a personal intro): apply
  this voice only to the first-person sections; leave neutral sections neutral.
- **Enoevol brand copy vs. Matt personally:** if it's published under the studio
  name but reads first-person-plural or founder-voiced, still apply this; if
  it's neutral product/marketing copy, don't.
- **Unsure whether it's "as Matt"?** Ask one short question rather than
  guessing: "Want this in your voice, or neutral?"
- **Content ideas from design-signal-scout** (content-opportunity-generator):
  when drafting those into actual posts or captions, this skill governs the
  final wording.
